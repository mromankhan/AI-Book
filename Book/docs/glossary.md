# Glossary of Terms

This glossary defines key terms used throughout the "Physical AI: Humanoid & Robotics Systems" book.

## A

**Actuator**: A device that converts electrical energy into mechanical motion in a robot.

**Artificial Intelligence (AI)**: The simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer systems.

## B

**Balance Control**: The process of maintaining a robot's center of mass within its support polygon to prevent falling.

**Center of Mass (CoM)**: The average location of the mass of an object, critical for balance in robotics.

**Control System**: A system that manages, commands, directs, or regulates the behavior of other devices or systems.

## D

**Digital Twin**: A virtual representation of a physical system that mirrors its real-world counterpart in real-time.

**Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent movements that a mechanical system can make.

**Dynamics**: The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of bodies under the action of forces.

## E

**Embodiment**: The concept of an AI system having a physical form that interacts with the physical world.

**Embodied AI**: Artificial intelligence systems that interact with the physical world through a physical body.

**End Effector**: The device at the end of a robot arm designed to interact with the environment.

## F

**Field of View (FoV)**: The extent of the observable world that is seen at any given moment by a sensor or camera.

**Finite State Machine (FSM)**: A computational model composed of a finite number of states, transitions between states, and actions.

## H

**Humanoid Robot**: A robot with human-like form and characteristics.

## I

**Inverse Kinematics**: The use of algorithms to determine the joint parameters that achieve a desired position of the end-effector.

**Inverted Pendulum**: A classic control problem where a pole is balanced above a pivot point, applicable to bipedal balance.

**Iterative Learning Control (ILC)**: A control technique that improves system performance by repeating a task and adapting the control input based on previous errors.

## J

**Joint**: A connection between two or more links of a robot, allowing relative motion between them.

## L

**LIDAR**: Light Detection and Ranging, a remote sensing method that uses pulsed laser light to measure distances.

**Locomotion**: The act of moving from place to place, especially used in robotics for walking, rolling, or other movement systems.

## M

**Machine Learning**: The study of computer algorithms that improve automatically through experience and by the use of data.

**Manipulator**: A device that manipulates objects in a controlled fashion, usually consisting of joints and links.

**Morphological Computation**: The use of body dynamics in a task to reduce computational effort for the controller.

## N

**Navigation**: The process of planning, recording, and controlling the movement of a robot from one location to another.

## P

**Perception**: The ability of a robot to interpret sensory data to understand its environment.

**PID Controller**: A control loop mechanism employing Proportional, Integral, and Derivative feedback to control systems.

**Physical AI**: The field of artificial intelligence focused on systems that interact with the physical world through their physical form.

**Point Cloud**: A collection of data points defining the shape of an object, typically captured by 3D imaging systems.

## R

**Reinforcement Learning**: A type of machine learning where agents learn to make decisions by performing actions and receiving rewards or penalties.

**Robotics**: The interdisciplinary branch of engineering and science that includes mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, computer science, and others.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it.

**Sensor**: A device that detects or measures a physical property and records, indicates, or otherwise responds to it.

**Sensors, Actuators, Controllers**: The three fundamental components of any robot, allowing it to perceive, act, and control respectively.

**Simulation**: The imitation of the operation of a real-world process or system over time, especially important in robotics for safe testing.

**State Estimation**: The process of determining the state of a system from noisy and incomplete measurements.

## T

**TensorFlow**: An open-source library for machine learning and deep neural networks developed by Google.

**Trajectory**: The path that a moving object follows through space as a function of time.

## U

**Unity (Game Engine)**: A cross-platform game engine used for creating simulations and virtual environments for robotics.

## V

**Vision System**: A computer system that processes visual information to recognize, measure, or control objects.

## W

**Wheelchair Robotics**: The application of robotics technology to improve the functionality and autonomy of wheelchairs.

**Whole-Body Control**: A robotics control approach that considers the dynamics and constraints of the entire robot body simultaneously.
